# Topic Cluster Methodology

This document defines the methodology for generating strategic content architecture with pillar topics and subtopics.

## Pillar Topic Selection

### What Makes a Good Pillar Topic?

**Criteria:**
1. **Broad but bounded:** Wide enough for 5-10 subtopics, narrow enough to be coherent
2. **Audience-relevant:** Addresses a core need or interest of your target audience
3. **Category-aligned:** Fits within your product category and expertise
4. **Differentiation-friendly:** Allows you to showcase unique value

**Examples:**

**Good Pillars:**
- "AI-Powered Sprint Planning" (bounded, differentiation-aligned)
- "Remote Team Collaboration" (audience-relevant, specific)
- "Project Management for Engineering Teams" (audience-specific, bounded)

**Bad Pillars:**
- "Project Management" (too broad, no differentiation)
- "Tips and Tricks" (vague, no strategic value)
- "Blog Posts" (not a topic, just a format)

### Pillar Topic Framework

**Three pillar types to balance:**

1. **Category Pillars** (table stakes)
   - Cover fundamental topics in your category
   - Example: "Project Management Fundamentals"
   - Purpose: Establish baseline authority

2. **Audience Pillars** (relevance)
   - Address specific audience pain points
   - Example: "Remote Team Workflows"
   - Purpose: Demonstrate understanding of audience needs

3. **Differentiation Pillars** (competitive advantage)
   - Showcase unique features or positioning
   - Example: "AI-Powered Features"
   - Purpose: Highlight what makes you different

**Recommended mix:** 1-2 category pillars, 2-3 audience/differentiation pillars.

## Subtopic Generation

### Subtopic Criteria

**Good subtopics:**
- Answer specific user questions
- Cover different aspects of the pillar
- Range from beginner to advanced
- Include multiple content types (how-to, comparison, conceptual)

**Subtopic types to include:**

1. **Conceptual** ("What is X?", "Why X matters")
2. **Comparative** ("X vs Y", "When to use X")
3. **Tactical** ("How to do X", "Step-by-step X")
4. **Strategic** ("X best practices", "Common X mistakes")
5. **Evidence** ("X case studies", "X benchmarks")

### Subtopic Depth Levels

**Beginner:**
- "What is sprint planning?"
- "Sprint planning basics"

**Intermediate:**
- "How to run effective sprint planning meetings"
- "Sprint planning for distributed teams"

**Advanced:**
- "Optimizing sprint planning with AI"
- "Sprint planning at scale (10+ teams)"

**Why depth matters:** Beginners need foundational content, experts need advanced tactics. A cluster with only beginner content won't retain engaged users.

## Priority Scoring

### Priority Formula

**Priority = (Relevance × 2) + (Differentiation × 2) - Competition**

**Relevance (0-5):**
- 5 = Core audience pain point
- 3 = Moderate interest
- 1 = Nice-to-have

**Differentiation (0-5):**
- 5 = Showcases unique value
- 3 = Moderate differentiation
- 1 = Table stakes

**Competition (0-5):**
- 5 = Saturated (everyone covers this)
- 3 = Moderate competition
- 1 = Underserved

**Priority thresholds:**
- Score ≥ 12 = 🔴 HIGH
- Score 8-11 = 🟡 MEDIUM
- Score ≤ 7 = ⚪ LOW

### Strategic Prioritization

**High-priority topics should:**
- Address underserved audience needs
- Showcase differentiation
- Have low competition

**Example:**
- "AI sprint planning for remote teams"
  - Relevance: 5 (remote teams are core audience)
  - Differentiation: 5 (AI is unique value)
  - Competition: 2 (few competitors cover this)
  - Score: (5×2) + (5×2) - 2 = 18 → 🔴 HIGH

**Low-priority topics are:**
- Table stakes (everyone covers them)
- Low differentiation
- High competition

**Example:**
- "What is project management?"
  - Relevance: 4 (foundational knowledge)
  - Differentiation: 1 (no unique angle)
  - Competition: 5 (saturated)
  - Score: (4×2) + (1×2) - 5 = 5 → ⚪ LOW

## Execution Planning

### Content Creation Order

**Phase 1: High-Priority Topics (Weeks 1-4)**
- Create 3-5 high-priority pieces
- Focus on differentiation and audience needs
- These are your competitive advantages

**Phase 2: Medium-Priority Topics (Weeks 5-8)**
- Fill out pillar coverage
- Balance depth and breadth
- Connect to high-priority content

**Phase 3: Low-Priority Topics (Weeks 9-12)**
- Complete table-stakes content
- Ensure comprehensive coverage
- Optimize existing content

### Content Format Recommendations

**Hub Pages (Pillar Pages):**
- Comprehensive overview of the pillar topic
- 2000-3000 words
- Links to all subtopic content
- Example: "Complete Guide to AI-Powered Sprint Planning"

**Subtopic Pages:**
- Deep dive into specific aspect
- 1000-1500 words
- Links back to hub page
- Example: "How to Set Up AI Sprint Planning in 5 Steps"

**Supporting Content:**
- Case studies, tutorials, comparisons
- 500-1000 words
- Links to relevant subtopics
- Example: "Case Study: How Acme Reduced Sprint Planning Time by 50%"

## Cluster Maintenance

### When to Update Clusters

**Quarterly review:**
- Add new topics based on content opportunities
- Reprioritize based on performance data
- Archive low-performing topics

**Triggers for updates:**
- New product features launched
- Competitor launches new content
- Audience needs shift
- Search trends change

### Cluster Evolution

**Mature clusters:**
- All high-priority content created
- Focus shifts to optimization and updates
- Add advanced/niche subtopics

**New clusters:**
- Emerging audience needs
- New product categories
- Competitive gaps identified

## Limitations

### Free Tier Limitations
- **LLM-generated clusters:** Based on brand context only, no external data
- **No search volume data:** Priority based on strategic judgment, not search metrics
- **No competitor analysis:** Can't see what competitors are ranking for

- **Tavily competitor data:** See what competitors rank for
- **Search volume insights:** Prioritize by actual search demand
- **Historical tracking:** Track cluster performance over time
